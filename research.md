## Case Studies

### Louis Vuitton Monogram
The interlocking L and V create a single unified glyph — two letters that cannot be separated without destroying the mark. The symmetry is bilateral: the composition mirrors across a vertical axis, creating visual stability that reads as permanence. The monogram tiles into a repeating pattern (the famous canvas), proving the mark works at any scale and in any arrangement. **Key lesson:** Interlocking forms signal inseparability — two entities becoming one.

### Apple Logo
Rob Janoff's apple uses proportions derived from circular geometry (often attributed to the golden ratio, though debated). The bite provides asymmetry that prevents confusion with a cherry or generic fruit — it's a *functional* detail disguised as a design choice. The silhouette is recognizable at 16px or 16 feet. No text needed. **Key lesson:** A single, mathematically clean silhouette with one deliberate imperfection creates instant recognition.

### Nike Swoosh
Carolyn Davidson's mark is a single stroke — one gesture that implies motion, speed, and the wing of Nike (goddess of victory). There are no enclosed shapes, no symmetry, no detail. It works because it's the simplest possible expression of forward movement. It cost $35 and became worth billions. **Key lesson:** Reduce to the single essential gesture. Motion > stillness.

### Gucci Double-G
Mirror-symmetric interlocking G's, derived from founder Guccio Gucci's initials. The forms face each other like a Rorschach test — this bilateral symmetry triggers deep psychological responses (faces, eyes, gates). The interlocking is tight; the letters share strokes. The serif weight communicates heritage and luxury. **Key lesson:** Mirror symmetry with shared geometry creates a sense of guarded exclusivity.

### Palantir Logo
A geometric construction based on overlapping circles forming a pointed almond shape (vesica piscis) — referencing the palantír seeing-stones from Tolkien. The mark is entirely constructible with compass and straightedge, communicating mathematical precision and inevitability. It feels *discovered*, not designed. **Key lesson:** Geometric construction from first principles signals intelligence and permanence.

### Stripe Logo
A clean sans-serif wordmark where the italicized "S" carries all the personality. The logotype uses restrained, custom-tuned type — no icon needed. The distinctive S-curve echoes the flow of money, data, transactions. Color is used minimally (the gradient purple accent is secondary to the wordmark). **Key lesson:** A wordmark can be an icon if one letter carries enough personality.

## Extracted Principles

| Principle | Rule |
|---|---|
| **Proportion** | Use mathematical relationships (golden ratio, grid systems, circle packing). Logos that feel "right" are usually geometrically constructed. |
| **Symmetry** | Bilateral symmetry = stability, trust, premium. Asymmetry = motion, disruption. Choose deliberately. |
| **Negative Space** | The space *inside* and *around* the mark carries meaning (FedEx arrow, Carrefour C). Hidden details reward closer inspection and create brand lore. |
| **Scalability** | Must be recognizable as a 16×16 favicon and on a billboard. This demands simplicity — if it doesn't work at 16px, it's too complex. |
| **Color Restraint** | The best marks work in pure black and white first. Color is a layer added on top, never a crutch. One accent color maximum in primary usage. |
| **Emotional Response** | A logo must trigger a feeling in under 200ms — before conscious thought. Premium marks feel inevitable, like they couldn't have been anything else. |
| **Hidden Meaning** | Layers of meaning (Amazon's A→Z arrow = everything + a smile, Palantir's Tolkien reference) create depth. The logo becomes a story. |

---

## The Meta-Rule

Every iconic logo follows one pattern: **reduction to essence**. Strip away everything that isn't load-bearing. What remains is the mark. If you can remove something and the logo still works, it shouldn't have been there.
